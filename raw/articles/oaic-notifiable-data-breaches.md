# OAIC Notifiable Data Breaches Scheme

**Source:** Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC)  
**Governing Law:** Privacy Act 1988 (Cth), Part IIIC  
**Reporting:** Quarterly statistics published by OAIC

## Overview

The Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) scheme requires eligible data breaches to be reported to the OAIC and affected individuals. The scheme operates under the Privacy Act and covers Australian Privacy Principles (APP) entities.

## What Must Be Notified

An **eligible data breach** occurs when:
- There is unauthorised access to, or disclosure of, personal information
- The access or disclosure would likely result in serious harm to any of the individuals to whom the information relates
- The entity has failed to take remedial steps to prevent the risk of serious harm

**Assessment period:** If unsure whether a breach is eligible, entities have 30 days to assess.

## Supply Chain Dimensions

The OAIC has explicitly highlighted supply chain risks in its data breach reporting:
- **Third-party providers** responsible for significant proportion of reportable breaches
- Data shared with service providers must be covered by contractual obligations
- Entities remain accountable for breaches occurring through third-party processors

## Recent Statistics (2024-2025)

- **Jul-Dec 2024:** 595 breaches reported (15% increase over prior period)
- **Jan-Jun 2025:** 532 breaches reported (10% decrease)
- **Top sectors:** Health, finance, government, retail
- **Leading cause:** Malicious or criminal attack (approx. 67%)

## OAIC Guidance on Supply Chain

The OAIC's data breach report specifically calls out that organisations must:
- Conduct due diligence on third-party service providers
- Include data breach notification obligations in contracts
- Assess fourth-party risks (sub-processors)
- Maintain oversight of how data is handled across the supply chain
