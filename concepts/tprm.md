# TPRM — Third Party Risk Management

## Definition

Third Party Risk Management (TPRM) is the discipline of identifying, assessing, mitigating, and monitoring risks that arise from an organisation's relationships with external vendors, suppliers, service providers, and partners (fourth parties).

## TPRM Lifecycle

1. **Onboarding / Due Diligence** — Assess risk before engagement
2. **Tiering / Classification** — Categorise vendors by criticality and risk exposure
3. **Contractual Controls** — SLAs, audit rights, termination provisions, security requirements
4. **Ongoing Monitoring** — Continuous or periodic reassessment
5. **Offboarding / Exit** — Data return/destruction, transition planning

## Key Risk Categories

- **Operational risk** — service provider failure disrupts operations
- **Cyber risk** — third-party system is attack vector
- **Data risk** — personal information mishandled by third party
- **Concentration risk** — over-reliance on single vendor for critical function
- **Fourth-party risk** — sub-processors and sub-subcontractors
- **Regulatory risk** — non-compliance by third party creates liability

## Australia Context

Australia's TPRM market is shaped by:

- **CPS 230 (2025)** — mandates TPRM programs for all APRA-regulated entities
- **SOCI Act** — requires risk management programs covering supply chain for 11 critical sectors
- **NDB Scheme** — organisations liable for data breaches through third-party processors
- **ASIC expectations** — focuses on operational resilience for market intermediaries

## Market Statistics

- Global TPRM market: USD 7.42B (2023) → USD 20.59B (2030), ~12.7% CAGR
- Asia-Pacific TPRM market: USD 2.16B (2025) → USD 8.47B (2033)
- Australian ASIC survey: 44% of organisations manage third-party risk poorly
- McGrathNicol/YouGov (Aug 2024): 82% of Australian companies do not extend risk assessments beyond Tier 1 suppliers

## TPRM vs Traditional Vendor Management

Traditional vendor management focuses on contract compliance and SLAs. TPRM extends this to:
- Proactive risk identification before engagement
- Continuous monitoring vs point-in-time assessments
- Fourth-party visibility
- Concentration risk analysis
- Scenario analysis for provider failure
